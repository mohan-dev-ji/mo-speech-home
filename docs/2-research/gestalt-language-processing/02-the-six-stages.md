# 2 · The six stages of Natural Language Acquisition

*Blanc's NLA framework, stage by stage, with examples — and a running note on what each stage asks of an AAC tool.*

---

A quick orientation before the detail. The six stages describe **one continuous journey**: from language that is *borrowed whole* to language that is *built fresh*. A real child is rarely tidily "in" one stage — they often show two or three at once, and progress is uneven. Treat the stages as a map of the terrain, not a staircase with clean steps. (This messiness is, in fact, one of the academic critiques — see [doc 5](05-evidence-and-references.md). For design purposes the map is still useful.)

Throughout, the adult's role is the same shape: **model, don't test.** You expose rich language at or just above the child's level. You do not quiz ("What's this? Say *go*"). Interrogation reliably shuts gestalt processors down.

---

## Stage 1 — Delayed echolalia (whole gestalts)

**What it looks like.** The child reproduces meaningful chunks of language they've heard before — from a parent, a sibling, a TV show, a song — without analysing or breaking them down. The chunk is used voluntarily and spontaneously in a *new* situation.

**Examples.** A child says *"Let's get out of here"* (picked up from an older brother) when they want to leave. Or *"You okay?"* in a moment of distress. The literal words may not match the situation — the *intonation and the felt meaning* do.

**The crucial point.** The meaning lives in the **melody**, not the words. The same string said with different intonation can mean different things. This is why **prosody is not decoration at Stage 1 — it is the message.**

**Adult role.** Acknowledge it as real communication. Recognising that the child *is communicating* matters more than decoding the literal intent. This validation helps the child see themselves as a communicator.

**What it asks of an AAC tool.** Instant access to *whole phrases* (not just words), spoken with rich, natural intonation. A board that only offers single words has nothing to give a Stage 1 child.

---

## Stage 2 — Mitigation (mix and match)

**What it looks like.** The child begins to break their Stage 1 gestalts apart and recombine the pieces into new phrases. Blanc calls this **mitigation** — the "mix and match" of remembered fragments.

**Examples.** A child who knows *"Let's get out of here"* and *"Want some more"* produces a new blend: *"Let's get more."* They might also alter the intonation, turn a statement into a question (*"Want some more?"*), or trim a long chunk to a shorter one.

**The crucial point.** This is the first visible sign of *analytical* ability — the child is now operating on language, not just reproducing it. It is also often when speech suddenly becomes **more intelligible to other people** for the first time.

**Adult role.** Recognise mitigations as progress. Distinguish a true mitigation (a *changed* version of a known gestalt) from a brand-new gestalt (an *unchanged* borrowed phrase). Support without explicit teaching.

**What it asks of an AAC tool.** The ability to take a stored phrase and *modify or recombine* it — swap a word, shorten it, change a piece. This is exactly where "predictive / pop-up next words" navigation earns its keep: the tool offers the fragments the child might mix in next.

---

## Stage 3 — Single words and two-word combinations

**What it looks like.** The child isolates individual meaningful words out of their larger chunks, and starts producing short two-word combinations.

**Examples.** From *"Let's get out of here,"* the child extracts and uses *"get"* or *"out"* on its own. Or combines *"want" + "more."*

**The crucial point.** This is a cognitive turning point — language becomes **referential** and freely available across contexts. Blanc sometimes calls it a "corpus callosum moment." Watch out: the child's average utterance length may *drop* here (from whole phrases down to single words). To an untrained eye this looks like regression. **It is the opposite — it is the language breaking open into reusable parts.**

**Adult role.** Validate the single words. Explicitly reassure families/teachers that a shorter utterance here is *progress*, not decline.

**What it asks of an AAC tool.** Now single-word access matters — but it must coexist with the phrase access from Stages 1–2, not replace it. The child needs *both* the old whole gestalts and the new individual words available at once.

---

## Stage 4 — Beginning / simple grammar

**What it looks like.** The child starts assembling original sentences with basic grammar — moving from isolated words toward subject–verb–object structure.

**Examples.** From *"marker, marker"* to *"I got a yellow marker"* or *"I got two yellows."* This stage brings in **basic verb tenses, simple past and future ("I'm gonna get yellow"), pronouns, and the conjunction "and."**

**The crucial point.** Stage 4 is long and broad — it spans from first word-combinations all the way through basic sentences. Children often become noticeably **disfluent** here (hesitations, restarts, repeated words — "linguistic mazing"). This is **normal and expected** as they wrestle with new grammar; it is not stuttering and not a setback.

**Adult role.** Focus on *meaning*, not grammar correction. Model language just slightly above their current level. Normalise the disfluency for worried parents.

**What it asks of an AAC tool.** This is where your SLP's **tense / plural** requests begin to bite. The tool now needs to help inflect words — past/present/future, singular/plural — *without* forcing the child to hunt across pages. Hold-to-inflect on a symbol is a natural fit.

---

## Stage 5 — Complex grammar

**What it looks like.** Sophisticated sentences with multiple clauses and a wider range of connectors.

**Examples.** *"I got a yellow marker, but I need the red one too."* *"Why can't I have that?"* *"Not till tomorrow."* Lots more conjunctions, the full set of WH-questions (who/what/where/when/why/how), and varied ways of joining clauses.

**Adult role.** Keep modelling rich language in meaningful contexts; invite increasingly complex exchanges.

**What it asks of an AAC tool.** **Comparatives and superlatives** (*big / bigger / biggest*), richer connectors, question-word access. The grammar engine your SLP described is fully exercised here.

---

## Stage 6 — Advanced, fully integrated language

**What it looks like.** Fully flexible sentences with advanced grammar and nuance — temporal, conditional and abstract relationships.

**Examples.** *"Before I get the red marker, I need to find the blue one."* *"If you don't want me to, then why did you bring me?"*

**Adult role.** Shift toward confidence, abstract topics, and the social *use* of language.

**What it asks of an AAC tool.** At this point the learner may be ready for **free composition** — which is precisely your SLP's **keyboard page** request. A child who has travelled all six stages may want to type, not tap symbols. (And the humbling reason this matters: a non-verbal child *cannot tell you* they're ready for the keyboard. The tool has to make it discoverable — see [doc 4](04-glp-in-mo-speech.md).)

---

## The whole journey, mapped to your feature list

| NLA stage | Dominant need | Which "non-negotiable" it powers |
|---|---|---|
| 1 · Delayed echolalia | Whole phrases + rich intonation | **Tone / multiple audio** |
| 2 · Mitigation | Modify & recombine phrases | **Predictive pop-up navigation (GLP)** |
| 3 · Single words | Words *and* phrases together | Bridges to core-word grids |
| 4 · Simple grammar | Tense, plurals | **Hold-to-inflect: past/present/future, plurals** |
| 5 · Complex grammar | Comparatives, connectors, WH-Qs | **Comparatives / superlatives / morphemes** |
| 6 · Advanced language | Free composition | **Keyboard page** |

Six of the seven non-negotiables fall straight out of the six stages. The seventh — **retaining English words in a Hindi board** — is orthogonal (it's about bilingualism, not GLP) but compatible, and is handled in [doc 4](04-glp-in-mo-speech.md).

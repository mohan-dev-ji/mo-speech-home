# 5 · The evidence picture, and references

*Read before you cite GLP to a clinician, a funder, or in marketing. The framework is clinically popular but academically contested — knowing both sides protects you.*

---

## The honest summary

GLP / NLA sits in an unusual place: **beloved in clinical practice, thin and contested in the research literature.** Both things are true at once, and a credible product has to hold both.

- **In practice:** Mainstream. Taught, certified (Meaningful Speech), and expected by working SLPs — including the one advising you. Building for it is responding to genuine, current professional demand.
- **In research:** A 2024 peer-reviewed critical analysis (Hutchins, Knight & Prelock, in *Autism & Developmental Language Impairments*) argues the framework's empirical foundation is weak. You should know its specific claims so you're never caught overclaiming.

## What the critique actually says

From the 2024 critical analysis (full citation below):

- **No controlled evidence.** Support for NLA is, in the authors' words, "entirely anecdotal" and vulnerable to confirmation and survivor bias. There are no peer-reviewed controlled trials.
- **The headline statistics are unsourced.** Widely-shared social-media claims that "50–85% of autistic children are gestalt processors" lack identifiable sources and rest on circular logic.
- **The stages may not be discrete.** Analysis of NLA's own example children shows them occupying several "stages" at once, undercutting the idea of a clean staged progression. The authors call them "pseudostages" imposed on what is really continuous development.
- **The GLP label itself is questioned.** They argue "gestalt language processor" "may not be a legitimate clinical entity," and that *all* language learners use chunk-based parsing — so a hard analytic-vs-gestalt split may not carve nature at its joints.
- **Assessment isn't validated.** NLA's stage-coding has, to their knowledge, never been tested for inter-rater reliability.
- **Some specific advice conflicts with evidence**, e.g. avoiding single words or verbs at certain stages doesn't square with existing research on effective language input.

## What even the critics concede

Importantly, the same critics credit the GLP movement with real, valuable contributions:

- Raising awareness that **echolalia is communicative**, not meaningless.
- Promoting **following the child's lead**, responsivity, and honouring a child's preferred mode of communication.
- Advancing **neurodiversity-affirming** practice.

Their caveat: these humane practices are *not new* and weren't invented by NLA — they predate it. But they are sound, and they are the parts Mo Speech most relies on.

## What this means for you — practically

1. **Build for it.** Your users and their clinicians want it; the underlying humane principles are well-founded; and the *features* (phrases, prosody, gradual reveal, inflection) are good AAC design regardless of whether the six-stage theory is perfectly validated.
2. **Don't theory-lock the product.** Favour flexible primitives — phrases, words, inflections, prosody, gating — over hard-coding "Blanc's six stages" as an immovable spine. If the field's consensus shifts, your features still stand. This is *also* just good engineering.
3. **Market the outcomes, not the dogma.** Say "supports children who learn language in whole phrases" or "grows from first words to full sentences," not "scientifically proven six-stage method." Describe GLP as a *respected clinical framework we support*, never as settled science. That phrasing is both honest and defensible to a sceptical funder or an academic.
4. **Lean on your clinical advisors as validators.** Your SLP and your native-speaker reviewers are exactly the credibility you need — real practitioners who want these features for real clients. That's a stronger claim than citing a contested protocol.
